Meet the Knurled2Nut Driver

This page is a write up with information on how to customize, print, and use this 3D printed knurled nut driver bit. This bit is unlike others in that it is designed to be used with a power tool. It does not have any When using the proper driver, it has almost no risk of "over-tightening" or "over-torque-ing" the nuts and destroying a jack because it is designed to slip rather instead of over tightening.




Other People's Designs

Traditionally, most designs rely on two little "slots" or "notches" on the knurled nut. A tool slides into these "slots", the same way a screwdriver slides into a horizontal ("flathead") or cross ("Phillips") slot on a screw. Sometimes it is hard to line up with these "slots", or you will slip out of the notches if you are not holding the tool perpendicular enough, so some tools also provide a center alignment pin which goes into the 3.5mm jack itself.

You can find a list of alternative tools at the bottom of the README of the GitHub repository.

The Design

Initially, I designed a bit to also fit in these "slots". However, these were quickly destroyed when using a power tool... and the whole point of this design is to be able to use it with a power tool. You will find many people recommending not to do this in various places on the internet. We have been successfully using this bit with a power tool in production.

Instead, of utilizing the slots on a knurled nut, this bit is designed to use the force of friction. You can see in the image on the left that the bit has a "cone-shaped" cutout. As you push down on the knurled nut, this cone squeezes down. The concept is that simple, and it works.

Because the bit does not use the slots of the knurled nut, it is easy to align. It fits at any rotation. You don't need to be careful about slipping out of the slots. If you do happen to over-torque the nut, the bit simply spins rather than being locked into place with the nut (via the slots). This drastically reduces the chance for destroying your jacks with too much torque. It is designed to damage the bit rather than the nut.

The risk of damage to your Eurorack faceplate is lessened as well. Because the bit is made of plastic (I am printing with PLA), you don't have as much risk of scratching the faceplate with a metal driver.

Knurled... squared?

After some weeks of testing... I have made some improvements to the driver to reduce the rate at which it wears. I added some "teeth". To do this I created a cylinder. In a for loop, I then subtracted a triangle at different orientations. In other words, imagine you're making a simple circle cookie... or pizza! Then, you cut out a triangle with a knife, going almost to the edges. Then do that again, but first, rotate your triangle 10 degrees. If you're rotating by 10 degrees, to go all the way around you'll need to make (360 degrees / 10 degrees = ) 36 triangle cutouts. What you'll be left with is a bunch of ridges on the inside.
